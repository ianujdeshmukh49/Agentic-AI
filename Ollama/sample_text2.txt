

**The Country That Decided to Measure Happiness**

In 1972, a young king came to power in one of the most isolated nations on earth. His name was Jigme Singye Wangchuck, he was sixteen years old, and he had just inherited the throne of Bhutan — a landlocked Buddhist kingdom tucked into the eastern Himalayas, sandwiched between the two most populous countries in the world, China and India, and almost entirely unknown to the rest of it.

Bhutan had no television. No internet. Barely any roads. Outsiders were not allowed in without special permission, and very few asked. The world had essentially forgotten this small country existed, which suited Bhutan just fine.

But the young king had a problem. The world was changing around him and eventually it would reach his mountains whether he invited it or not. Other developing nations were opening their borders to foreign money, foreign corporations, foreign culture — chasing something economists called GDP, Gross Domestic Product, the single number that the entire modern world had agreed would measure whether a country was succeeding or failing. More GDP meant progress. Less meant poverty. That was the deal.

Wangchuck looked at this and said, quietly, no.

Not because he was naive. Not because he wanted his people to stay poor. But because he had watched what happened to countries that chased GDP without asking what they were chasing it for. He had seen forests stripped for timber revenue. He had seen cultures flattened by tourism. He had seen ancient ways of living dissolve in a single generation in exchange for things that did not actually make people feel better.

So he proposed something that the economics world initially laughed at.

He called it Gross National Happiness.

The idea was simple and radical at the same time. Instead of measuring only how much a country produced, Bhutan would measure how its people actually felt. It would track nine domains — living standards yes, but also psychological wellbeing, time use, cultural resilience, community vitality, ecological diversity, good governance, health, and education. A country could score well on some and poorly on others and the government would be obligated to respond to the ones that were failing. GDP would be one input among many, not the entire scoreboard.

The world laughed for about thirty years.

Then 2008 happened. The global financial crisis. Countries with the highest GDPs on earth nearly collapsed because the number had been measuring activity, not health. Banks were productive right until the moment they destroyed everything. The GDP of the United States went up every time someone got sick and paid for treatment. It went up when a forest fire was large enough to require an emergency response. It went up when a marriage broke down and two lawyers got paid. The number was agnostic to whether the activity it was counting was good or bad for actual human lives. It just counted.

Suddenly Bhutan did not look so naive.

The United Nations passed a resolution in 2011 inviting member countries to measure the happiness of their people and use it in public policy. France commissioned a report. The OECD created a Better Life Index. The World Happiness Report began publishing annually, ranking countries not by output but by how their citizens reported feeling about their lives. The conversation Wangchuck had started alone in the Himalayas in 1972 had quietly become a global one.

But here is where it gets complicated and interesting.

Bhutan itself does not rank particularly high on the World Happiness Report. It sits somewhere in the middle, not near the top where you might expect it given that it invented the whole framework. The Scandinavian countries dominate — Finland, Denmark, Iceland — places with high GDP, strong institutions, generous welfare systems, low corruption. Which raises an uncomfortable question. Did Bhutan get it wrong? Or did everyone else get it right for the wrong reasons?

The researchers who study this say something nuanced. They say happiness at a national level is not one thing. There is evaluative happiness — when you step back and assess your life and decide it is good. There is experienced happiness — how you actually feel moment to moment, whether there is joy or anxiety or boredom in your Tuesday afternoon. And there is what the psychologist Martin Seligman calls flourishing — a deeper sense of meaning, engagement, relationships, accomplishment, that may have nothing to do with whether you felt good today.

Bhutan was never really measuring the first kind. It was trying to protect conditions for the third kind. And those are very different projects.

The forests of Bhutan are among the most protected on earth. The country is carbon negative — it absorbs more carbon than it produces, one of only a handful of countries that can say this. Cultural festivals have been preserved not as tourist attractions but as living practices. Monasteries still function. The pace of modernisation has been deliberately slowed, not to keep people poor, but to give culture time to absorb change rather than be replaced by it.

Whether that was worth it is a question Bhutanese people answer differently depending on who you ask and how old they are. Young people sometimes feel trapped. They see the world through phones now — Bhutan finally allowed television in 1999 — and some of what they see makes their own country feel small and slow. The tension between preservation and aspiration is real and unresolved.

Which maybe is exactly the point.

The most honest thing Gross National Happiness ever claimed was not that Bhutan had figured out happiness. It was that happiness was worth figuring out. That a government had an obligation to ask the question and keep asking it even when the answer was complicated. That the purpose of an economy was not to grow but to serve the people inside it, and that those were not the same thing.

In a world where everything from your productivity app to your country's budget is optimised for a number, there is something quietly radical about a sixteen year old king in the mountains deciding the number was wrong and spending the rest of his life trying to find a better one.

He is still alive. He is sixty eight now. Bhutan is still there, still counting what it counts, still trying.
